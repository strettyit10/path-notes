❤️ Section 1 — Congenital Heart Disease
Most CommonVentricular Septal Defect (VSD)
Classic NBME: infant with loud holosystolic murmur, heart failure symptoms, then pulmonary hypertension if untreated
Buzzwords
  • Holosystolic murmur at left lower sternal border
  • Failure to thrive, tachypnea
  • Small defect can be louder
  • Most common congenital heart defect
How tested
  • Left-to-right shunt question
  • Spontaneous closure in childhood
  • Eisenmenger development if untreated
  • Differentiate from MR
High-yield core

Presentation: infant CHF symptoms, murmur. Pathophys: LV → RV shunt increases pulmonary flow and RV volume load. Treatment: observe if small; surgical repair if large/symptomatic.

Trap

Holosystolic does not always mean MR. VSD is at the left lower sternal border; MR radiates to the axilla.

Split S2Atrial Septal Defect (ASD)
Buzzwords
  • Fixed split S2
  • Ostium secundum most common
  • Exercise intolerance in child
  • Often asymptomatic early
How tested
  • “Silent shunt” style question
  • Association with Down syndrome
  • Left-to-right shunt causing RA/RV enlargement

Presentation: often subtle in childhood. Pathophys: L→R shunt across atria causes right-sided volume overload. Treatment: closure if significant or symptomatic.

If you see → think

Fixed split S2 → ASD

ContinuousPatent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA)
Buzzwords
  • Continuous “machine-like” murmur
  • Bounding pulses
  • Wide pulse pressure
  • Prematurity or congenital rubella
How tested
  • Indomethacin closes it
  • PGE keeps it open in ductal-dependent lesions
  • Differentiate from AR by murmur type

Pathophys: aorta → pulmonary artery shunt. Treatment: indomethacin/ibuprofen to close; prostaglandin E to keep open if needed in cyanotic lesions.

Trap

Bounding pulses can also occur in aortic regurgitation. Murmur type separates them: continuous = PDA, diastolic = AR.

CyanoticTetralogy of Fallot (TOF)
Buzzwords
  • Cyanotic child
  • Squatting relieves symptoms
  • Tet spells with crying/exertion
  • DiGeorge association
How tested
  • PROVe mnemonic
  • Degree of pulmonic stenosis determines severity
  • Right-to-left shunt lesion

Components: Pulmonary stenosis, RV hypertrophy, Overriding aorta, VSD. Pathophys: RV outflow obstruction drives R→L shunting and cyanosis. Treatment: surgical repair.

If you see → think

Squatting child + cyanosis → TOF

Birth CyanosisTransposition of the Great Arteries (TGA)
Buzzwords
  • Immediate cyanosis at birth
  • No major murmur
  • Maternal diabetes association
  • Needs mixing lesion
How tested
  • Parallel rather than serial circulation
  • PGE used acutely
  • Survival requires PDA/ASD/VSD

Pathophys: aorta arises from RV and pulmonary artery from LV, producing parallel circulations. Treatment: prostaglandin E then surgical repair.

Trap

TGA gives immediate cyanosis and usually not the classic squatting history of TOF.

Pulse DifferentialCoarctation of the Aorta
Buzzwords
  • Upper extremity hypertension
  • Weak femoral pulses
  • Rib notching
  • Turner syndrome association
How tested
  • Arm-leg BP differential
  • Leg claudication in child/adolescent
  • Preductal vs postductal concept

Pathophys: aortic narrowing causes decreased distal perfusion and proximal hypertension. Treatment: surgical repair.

Outflow TractTruncus Arteriosus + TAPVR
Truncus arteriosus

Buzzwords: single great vessel, cyanosis, early heart failure, DiGeorge.
Pathophys: failure of outflow tract septation.
Think: one vessel = truncus.

TAPVR

Buzzwords: pulmonary veins drain to right atrium, cyanosis, ASD needed for survival.
Pathophys: oxygenated blood returns to right side instead of left.
Think: abnormal pulmonary venous drainage = TAPVR.

Plaque RuptureGeneral MI Essentials
Buzzwords
  • Crushing substernal chest pain
  • Radiation to jaw or left arm
  • Diaphoresis, nausea, dyspnea
  • Elevated troponin
How tested
  • Most common cause = plaque rupture with thrombosis
  • Different arteries map to different infarcts
  • Complication timing is heavily tested

Pathophys: atherosclerotic plaque rupture exposes thrombogenic core → platelet aggregation → thrombus → myocardial necrosis. Treatment: urgent reperfusion for STEMI plus aspirin, nitrates when appropriate, beta-blocker, statin, anticoagulation per scenario.

TimelineMI Histology and Rupture Window 
0–4 hr     : often no gross/light microscopic change
4–24 hr    : coagulative necrosis begins
1–3 days   : neutrophils dominate
3–7 days   : macrophages dominate → wall weakest → rupture risk highest
Weeks      : granulation tissue then dense fibrosis/scar
Most tested trap

3–7 days after MI is the classic rupture window because macrophages digest dead tissue before collagen scar has formed.

💪 Section 3 — Cardiomyopathies and Heart Failure
Most Common CMDilated Cardiomyopathy (DCM)
Buzzwords
  • Enlarged floppy heart
  • Low ejection fraction
  • Global hypokinesis
  • Alcohol, coxsackie, doxorubicin, Chagas
How tested
  • Most common cardiomyopathy
  • Systolic dysfunction question
  • Secondary MR/TR from dilation

Presentation: dyspnea, edema, orthopnea, fatigue. Pathophys: ventricular dilation causes impaired contractility and reduced EF. Treatment: standard HFrEF therapy.

If you see → think

Alcohol + low EF + enlarged heart → DCM

Athlete DeathHypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM)
Buzzwords
  • Young athlete collapse
  • Syncope with exertion
  • Asymmetric septal hypertrophy
  • Autosomal dominant sarcomere mutation
How tested
  • Sudden death in athletes
  • Obstructive physiology from thick septum
  • Differentiate from aortic stenosis

Pathophys: thickened septum obstructs LV outflow and causes diastolic dysfunction. Treatment: beta-blockers (and sometimes non-DHP CCBs depending on scenario).

Trap

Low EF points away from HCM and toward DCM. HCM is typically a diastolic dysfunction problem.

Stiff VentriclesRestrictive Cardiomyopathy (RCM)
Buzzwords
  • Normal EF
  • Diastolic dysfunction
  • Amyloidosis / hemochromatosis
  • Impaired filling
How tested
  • Heart failure with preserved EF
  • Differentiation from constrictive pericarditis
  • Infiltrative disease stem

Pathophys: noncompliant myocardium prevents ventricular filling while contractility is relatively preserved. Treatment: treat underlying cause when possible.

🔔 Section 4 — Valvular Disease
SADAortic Stenosis (AS)
Buzzwords
  • Syncope, angina, dyspnea
  • Crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur
  • Radiates to carotids
  • Elderly calcification or bicuspid valve
How tested
  • Exertional syncope
  • Pressure-overload LV hypertrophy
  • Differentiate from HCM

Pathophys: LV outflow obstruction creates pressure overload and concentric hypertrophy. Treatment: valve replacement for severe symptomatic disease.

Wide PPAortic Regurgitation (AR)
Buzzwords
  • Bounding pulses
  • Water-hammer pulse
  • Early diastolic murmur
  • Wide pulse pressure
How tested
  • Marfan / aortic root dilation
  • Endocarditis cause
  • Differentiate from PDA

Pathophys: diastolic backflow from aorta to LV causes LV volume overload and dilation. Treatment: valve surgery when severe.

HolosystolicMitral Regurgitation (MR)
Buzzwords
  • Holosystolic murmur
  • Radiates to axilla
  • Pulmonary edema
  • Papillary muscle rupture after MI
How tested
  • Post-MI complication
  • MVP progression
  • Differentiate from VSD

Pathophys: systolic backflow from LV to LA. Treatment: depends on cause/severity; surgery if severe.

Opening SnapMitral Stenosis (MS)
Buzzwords
  • Opening snap + diastolic rumble
  • Rheumatic fever history
  • Left atrial enlargement
  • Atrial fibrillation
How tested
  • Most common cause = rheumatic fever
  • Pulmonary hypertension from LA pressure rise

Pathophys: obstructed LA emptying increases LA pressure, leading to pulmonary congestion and later pulmonary hypertension. Treatment: valve repair/replacement depending on severity.

ClickMitral Valve Prolapse (MVP)
Buzzwords
  • Mid-systolic click
  • Young woman
  • Palpitations/anxiety
  • Marfan association
How tested
  • Myxomatous degeneration
  • Can progress to MR
  • Usually benign

Pathophys: myxomatous degeneration allows leaflet billowing into LA during systole. Treatment: often none unless significant MR or symptoms.

🦠 Section 5 — Infective Endocarditis

TypeTypical organismValve statusClassic clue
Acute endocarditisS. aureusCan infect normal valvesRapid, destructive infection; IV drug use

Buzzwords

Janeway lesions = painless
Osler nodes = painful
Splinter hemorrhages

Trap

Do not confuse rheumatic fever with infective endocarditis. Rheumatic fever is immune-mediated; endocarditis is infectious vegetations.

If you see → think

IV drug use → S. aureus
Dental procedure + abnormal valve → S. viridans

🫀 Section 6 — Pericardial Disease
Positional PainAcute Pericarditis
Buzzwords
  • Sharp chest pain
  • Worse lying down
  • Better leaning forward
  • Pericardial friction rub
How tested
  • Viral cause most common
  • Dressler after MI
  • Differentiate from infarction

Treatment: NSAIDs for many acute cases. Board pearl: positional chest pain strongly points away from MI and toward pericarditis.

EmergencyCardiac Tamponade
Buzzwords
  • Beck triad
  • Hypotension
  • JVD
  • Muffled heart sounds
  • Pulsus paradoxus
How tested
  • Free wall rupture after MI
  • Acute hemodynamic collapse
  • Requires urgent pericardiocentesis
Trap

A patient with sudden shock and distended neck veins after recent MI should make you think tamponade from free wall rupture, not ordinary heart failure.

ChronicConstrictive Pericarditis
Buzzwords
  • Rigid pericardium
  • Chronic impaired filling
  • Kussmaul sign
  • Mimics restrictive cardiomyopathy
How tested
  • Need to separate pericardial vs myocardial cause of filling restriction
  • Chronic rather than acute presentation

NBME Trap Checklist
Do not miss
  • Holosystolic murmur is not always MR — could be VSD
  • Bounding pulses can be PDA or AR — check murmur timing
  • Normal EF does not mean normal heart — think diastolic dysfunction
  • Stable angina is not an infarction
Classic traps
  • Early sudden death after MI = arrhythmia; later sudden death = rupture
  • Positional chest pain points to pericarditis, not MI
  • Immediate cyanosis points more toward TGA than TOF
  • Rheumatic fever causes MS classically, not infective vegetations
