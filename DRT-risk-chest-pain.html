🫀 Section 2 — Chest Pain / ACS
Initial TriageApproach to acute chest pain
Think broadly first, but ACS must be identified and triaged quickly because missed ACS has high short-term risk.
Classic ACS history
  • Abrupt onset
  • Pressure / squeezing / burning
  • Diffuse central or left chest discomfort
  • Can radiate to left arm or jaw
  • Often persistent, not just a pinpoint fleeting pain
  • Can be provoked by exertion / stress
What lowers ACS probability
  • Pinpoint pain
  • Sharp fleeting pain
  • Clearly pleuritic/positional only
  • Right-sided tearing pain suggests alternative dx
  • Normal exam does not rule it out
Pearl

Most acute chest pain visits do not end up being ACS, but the test-taking priority is recognizing the one that is.

Most Important Initial TestECG first, fast, and repeat when needed
Board answer

The most important initial diagnostic test in suspected ACS is the ECG.

  • Perform within 10 minutes of arrival
  • Used to rapidly triage STEMI vs non-ST-elevation ACS
  • ST elevation or new LBBB raises concern for STEMI
  • NSTE-ACS may show ST depression, T-wave inversion, or no changes
Common trap
  • Normal ECG does not exclude ACS
  • If symptoms continue or suspicion remains high, repeat the ECG
  • Troponin is extremely important, but it is not the first test to answer the initial triage question
Likely exam framing

“Patient presents with acute chest pain and concern for MI — what is the most important initial test?” → ECG, not troponin.

Serial Testing LogicRepeat ECG or troponin after 30 minutes?
This is exactly the kind of question that shows up because it tests whether you understand what changes fast and what changes slowly.
If you only get one answer

Repeat the ECG if ongoing concern and only 30 minutes have passed.

Reason: ECG changes can evolve quickly, while troponin usually takes a few hours to rise.

Troponin timing
  • Troponin is the gold-standard biomarker for myocardial necrosis
  • Begins to rise after about 4 hours
  • Stays elevated for several days
  • Use serial troponins, but not just 30 minutes later expecting a meaningful early rise
Take-home answer

At 30 minutes, if the question forces a choice, the better repeat test is the ECG, not troponin.

BiomarkersTroponin vs older biomarkers
What matters now
  • Troponin = most sensitive and specific marker for MI
  • High-sensitivity troponin is commonly used
  • It tells you there is myocardial necrosis
  • It does not by itself tell you the exact cause of cardiac injury
What is less useful
  • Myoglobin rises earlier but is not specific
  • CK/CK-MB is largely not used for primary ACS diagnosis in this lecture context
  • The exam is more likely to want troponin
If you see → think

“Most sensitive and specific marker for MI” → troponin.

Risk ScoresHEART / TIMI / GRACE — what they are really for
Correct use
  • Risk stratify
  • Estimate short-term risk of complications / major adverse cardiac events
  • Help determine admission vs follow-up vs invasive strategy
  • HEART score is useful when diagnosis remains uncertain
Incorrect use
  • Not the primary diagnostic test
  • Not intended to independently diagnose MI
  • Not a substitute for ECG + troponin + clinical judgment
Common trap

Risk scores are not for making the initial diagnosis of ACS. They are for risk stratification.

📉 Section 3 — CV Risk / Risk Reduction
FoundationPrimary vs secondary vs tertiary prevention
Definitions
  • Primary prevention: prevent disease/risk factor before it starts
  • Secondary prevention: identify disease early and treat
  • Tertiary prevention: prevent complications in those with disease
How lecture uses this
  • Established ASCVD/statin therapy is treated as secondary prevention
  • Lecture notes mention AHA can lump secondary + tertiary together in practice
  • Exam likely tests whether patient already has clinical ASCVD
Key Take-HomeThe 4 statin benefit groups

Frequently TestedWhat to do when statin decision is uncertain → CAC
When CAC is useful
  • Primary prevention patient age 40–75
  • No diabetes
  • LDL roughly 70–189
  • 10-year ASCVD risk borderline/intermediate
  • Uncertain statin decision or patient hesitant about starting/restarting statin
How to interpret
  • CAC = 0: can withhold/delay statin in selected patients
  • CAC 1–99: favors statin, especially if age >55
  • CAC ≥100 or ≥75th percentile: statin indicated
Classic exam version

Older patient with prior hyperlipidemia, no ASCVD/diabetes, hesitant to restart medication, and the question asks the next step under ACC/AHA → if the decision is uncertain, order a coronary artery calcium (CAC) scan.

Your ExampleStatin-hesitant 63-year-old woman — next step?
A 63-year-old woman has had high cholesterol in the past and previously took a statin but does not want to restart medication. She has no known coronary artery disease, no diabetes, and no other major risk factors given. According to ACC/AHA-style lipid guidance, what is the best next step?
A.Order an EKG
B ✓Order a coronary artery calcium scan
C.Order an Lp(a) level
D.Order a lipid profile
Why B is best

When the decision about statin therapy is uncertain or the patient is hesitant, the guideline supports using CAC to clarify risk and guide whether to start or restart statin therapy.

Why not the others?

EKG does not answer lipid-treatment uncertainty. Lp(a) is a risk-enhancing factor if measured, but CAC is the more direct “decision remains uncertain” test. A lipid profile is important generally, but in this style of question the tested next step is usually CAC.

Before Starting StatinBaseline labs / workup
Check before starting
  • Lipid panel
  • ALT or hepatic/liver panel
  • HbA1c or fasting glucose to identify/confirm diabetes
  • Consider secondary causes of hyperlipidemia
Secondary causes to think about
  • Medications
  • Smoking status
  • Family history
  • Very high triglycerides / other clinical context suggesting another driver
MonitoringWhat do you check after starting a statin?
This is another very likely exam question because it looks deceptively simple.
What to monitorWhenImportant exam note
Repeat lipid panel4–12 weeks after statin initiation or dose adjustment, then every 3–12 months as neededMain routine follow-up test
ALT / liver testingOnly if liver disease or significant side effectsNot routine serial testing for everyone
CK / CPKOnly if muscle pain / myalgiasNot routine
Glucose / HbA1cHelpful baseline assessment if indicatedQuestion-specific; not the routine “extra test” at statin follow-up
Likely answer style

If asked, “In addition to a lipid panel at follow-up, should ACC/AHA routine monitoring include CK, LFTs, CRP, glucose, etc.?” the best answer is usually no additional routine tests, unless there are symptoms or liver disease.

Your Example6-month statin follow-up question
In addition to a lipid panel after starting a statin, should routine follow-up include CK for muscle damage, LFTs, CRP, blood sugar testing, or no additional tests?
A.Creatine kinase for muscle damage
B.Liver function test
C.CRP for cardiac risk
D.Blood sugar testing for diabetes
E ✓No additional routine tests
Why E is best

Routine follow-up focuses on whether LDL falls appropriately. CK is only for muscle symptoms; liver testing only if liver disease/significant side effects. CRP is not routine monitoring. Glucose/HbA1c may be part of baseline risk assessment, but not the routine add-on follow-up answer here.

Intensity + Add-onsHigh-intensity statins and what happens when LDL stays high
Intensity pearl
  • Atorvastatin and rosuvastatin are the key high-intensity statins to know
  • High-intensity therapy should reduce LDL by ≥50%
  • If 10-year risk is ≥20%, target at least a 50% LDL reduction
When to add ezetimibe / PCSK9
  • Clinical ASCVD and LDL still ≥70 on maximally tolerated statin → consider ezetimibe
  • Very high-risk ASCVD and LDL still ≥70 on statin + ezetimibe → consider PCSK9 inhibitor
  • LDL ≥190 and still ≥100 on statin → ezetimibe is reasonable
Secondary PreventionPost-MI / clinical ASCVD patients
What to remember
  • Clinical ASCVD includes ACS, MI, stable/unstable angina, revascularization, stroke/TIA, PAD of atherosclerotic origin
  • These patients belong in the clinical ASCVD statin benefit group
  • Use high-intensity or maximally tolerated statin
  • Lecture notes specifically emphasize post-MI goal thinking of LDL ≤70; if still above 70 on maximal statin, consider ezetimibe
  • If statin intolerance develops: switch statin, lower dose, or consider ezetimibe depending on context

❓ Section 5 — Exam-Style Practice Questions
Question 1 — Most important initial test in ACS concern
A 58-year-old man presents with acute substernal chest pressure radiating to the left arm. He is diaphoretic and nauseated. Which is the most important initial diagnostic test?
A.Troponin
B ✓ECG
C.Chest CT
D.Stress test
E.HEART score
Why

ECG is the fastest way to determine whether the patient is having a STEMI and to guide immediate reperfusion decisions.

Question 2 — Repeat ECG or troponin at 30 minutes?
A patient presents 15 minutes after onset of chest pain. Initial ECG is nondiagnostic, initial troponin is normal, and symptoms continue 30 minutes later. Which is the better repeat test at that time?
A ✓Repeat ECG
B.Repeat troponin
C.CK-MB
D.Myoglobin
E.HEART score only
Why

ECG changes may evolve rapidly; troponin often requires hours to rise.

Question 3 — Statin-hesitant patient

B ✓Coronary artery calcium scan
C.Lp(a) level
D.Lipid panel only
E.Stress echocardiogram
Question 4 — Follow-up after adding a statin
A patient is started on a statin. At follow-up, in addition to a lipid panel, which additional test is recommended routinely in an asymptomatic patient?
A.CK
B.LFTs
C.CRP
D.HbA1c

🔥 Section 6 — Last-Minute Cram Sheet
CHEST PAIN
- Most important initial test in suspected ACS = ECG
- ECG should be done within 10 minutes
- Normal ECG does NOT rule out ACS
- Troponin = gold-standard biomarker for myocardial necrosis
- Troponin takes ~4 hours to rise
- Repeat ECG if symptoms continue and early ECG is nondiagnostic
- STEMI = complete occlusion
- NSTEMI = partial occlusion + positive troponin
- Unstable angina = no myocardial cell death, no troponin rise
- Risk scores stratify risk; they do NOT make the diagnosis
- STEMI -> immediate cath / reperfusion
- Thrombolytics: for STEMI when indicated; contraindicated in NSTE-ACS

CV RISK / RISK REDUCTION
- Know the 4 statin benefit groups cold
- Clinical ASCVD -> high-intensity or maximally tolerated statin
- LDL ≥190 -> high-intensity statin, no 10-year risk needed
- Diabetes age 40-75 with LDL ≥70 -> statin
- Primary prevention age 40-75, LDL 70-189, risk elevated -> discuss statin
- If statin decision uncertain -> CAC
- CAC 0 -> may defer statin in selected patients
- CAC 1-99 -> favors statin, especially age >55
- CAC ≥100 or ≥75th percentile -> statin indicated
- Baseline before statin: lipid panel, ALT, HbA1c/fasting glucose, consider secondary causes
- Follow-up lipid panel: 4–12 weeks after starting/changing statin
- Routine CK or LFT monitoring? No, unless symptoms/liver disease
- Clinical ASCVD + LDL still ≥70 on max statin -> add ezetimibe
