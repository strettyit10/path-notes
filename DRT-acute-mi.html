Section 1 - Master Rules
Know ColdThe 10 rules most likely to show up
First principles
  • First test in suspected ACS = ECG
  • Get the ECG within 10 minutes of first medical contact
  • EKG happens simultaneously with vitals, IV access, monitor, labs, and history
  • Repeat ECG if chest pain persists or presentation changes
  • Troponin rises after several hours; early normal value does not rule out MI
Management rules
  • STEMI = immediate reperfusion
  • Goal: cath lab / PCI within 90 min
  • If no timely cath lab, use thrombolytics and transfer
  • NSTE-ACS does not get fibrinolytics
  • Inferior MI + hypotension after nitro = think RV infarct
Section 2 - Inpatient ED Case: How to Work Up Possible MI
Case 1Initial presentation and differential
52-year-old man, tobacco history, wakes from a nap with mid-epigastric pain, nausea, diaphoresis, no relief with TUMS.
Tier 1 concern
  • STEMI / NSTEMI
  • Stable vs unstable angina
  • Pericarditis
Must rule out
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Aortic dissection/aneurysm
  • Less urgent GI causes such as reflux or gallbladder disease
Pearl

Epigastric pain with nausea and diaphoresis can still be ACS. Do not let the location trick you into assuming GI disease first.

BiomarkerTroponin
What to know
  • Most sensitive and specific marker for NSTE-ACS
  • Abundant in myocardium, virtually absent elsewhere
  • Rises within several hours of symptom onset
  • Can stay elevated for days to up to 2 weeks in larger MI
How it is tested
  • Initial negative value does not rule out very early MI
  • Serial troponins matter
  • Troponin separates NSTEMI from unstable angina
Case InterpretationInitial ECG and working diagnosis
  • No diagnostic ST elevation for STEMI
  • Left-axis deviation was present
  • T-wave inversions in I, aVL, V5-V6 and ST depression in V5-V6 were concerning
  • Initial hs-troponin around 1500 with normal creatinine
Working diagnosis

NSTEMI - chest pain + positive troponin + concerning ischemic ECG changes, but not enough ST elevation to call STEMI.

Classic trap

Statins matter for long-term plaque stabilization, but they are not the first "now-now" medication question in the first few minutes of an acute MI workup.

Section 3 - When a Stable NSTEMI Becomes a STEMI
ReassessmentAny change in symptoms = start over
In the lecture case, the patient improved, then developed renewed and more severe chest pain while waiting for admission.
  • Take new vitals
  • Obtain a new ECG
  • Repeat focused exam
  • Repeat troponin if indicated
  • Reassess sick vs not-sick
Rule

Any time there is a change in patient presentation, get new vitals and a new ECG.

STEMI ConversionWhat the second ECG showed
  • ST elevation in II, III, aVF
  • Reciprocal ST depression in anterior leads
  • This pattern localizes to an inferior MI, usually from the RCA
What changed?

The patient went from a more stable NSTEMI picture to an unstable inferior STEMI. At that point, medical stabilization is no longer enough.

FibrinolyticsAbsolute contraindication themes
  • Prior intracranial hemorrhage
  • Recent ischemic stroke
  • Known intracranial vascular lesion/neoplasm
  • Recent major head trauma or brain surgery
  • Active or recent serious bleeding
  • Suspected aortic dissection
How this is tested

They often ask whether you would give thrombolytics when PCI is unavailable. The answer depends on whether the patient has a major bleeding / intracranial contraindication.

Section 4 - Inferior MI, RCA, and Right Ventricular Infarct
Big TrapWhy nitroglycerin tanked the blood pressure
In the lecture case, nitroglycerin was given while the patient had an RCA infarct. Blood pressure then fell to about 70/palp and pain worsened.
What happened physiologically
  • RCA infarct can involve the right ventricle
  • Nitroglycerin causes venodilation and reduces preload
  • An ischemic RV depends on preload to maintain output
  • Preload drop = major fall in cardiac output and BP
What to do next
  • Think right ventricular infarct
  • Obtain a right-sided ECG
  • Avoid more nitrates
  • Support preload and hemodynamics
If you see -> think

Inferior STEMI + marked hypotension after nitro = RV infarct until proven otherwise.

Section 5 - Outpatient Case: Stable Angina vs ACS
Case 2The office patient with exertional chest pressure
58-year-old man, HTN, hyperlipidemia, family history of CAD, chest pressure climbing stairs for 6 months, resolves with rest, getting worse over time.
Why this sounds stable
  • Months-long pattern
  • Predictable with exertion
  • Relieved by rest
  • No immediate instability on initial office evaluation
What you still do first
  • Vitals
  • ECG
  • History + physical
  • Then decide clinic vs ED
Framework

The lecture emphasizes putting every chest-pain patient into one of four buckets: non-cardiac, chronic stable angina, possible ACS, or definite ACS.

TestingLeast invasive useful tests
  • Only order tests that can actually change what you do next
  • Possible labs: CBC, renal function, lipids, A1c, electrolytes, troponin depending on concern
  • CT angiogram can visualize coronary occlusion/atherosclerosis
  • Echocardiogram visualizes cardiac structure/function
  • Nuclear stress test compares rest vs stress perfusion
Case result

In the lecture case, CT angiogram showed partial RCA occlusion and nuclear stress testing showed decreased perfusion under stress, supporting stable angina.

Section 6 - Discharge and Long-Term Follow-Up
Post-MIWhat the patient leaves with
  • Non-invasive evaluation of ejection fraction
  • Lifestyle change counseling
  • Smoking cessation
  • Cardiac rehabilitation
  • Return-to-work / activity guidance
  • Discharge medications such as antiplatelet therapy and statin

Last-minute memory dump
- ECG first, within 10 min
- ECG repeats when symptoms change
- Troponin rises after several hours
- NSTEMI = positive troponin without STEMI-level ST elevation
- STEMI = complete occlusion until proven otherwise -> reperfusion now
- PCI preferred within 90 min
- NSTE-ACS does NOT get fibrinolytics
- Inferior MI = II, III, aVF
- Inferior/RCA infarct + nitro hypotension = RV infarct
- After MI: EF evaluation, rehab, lifestyle change, smoking cessation, meds
