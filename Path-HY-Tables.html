01
MI Histology Timeline
Source: Path-3R, Path-5R · Most tested window: 3–7 days
Rule: EARLY (<24h) = ELECTRICAL (V-fib). LATE (3–7d) = STRUCTURAL (ruptures). N for Neutrophils (1–3d), M for Macrophages (3–7d).
Timing Dominant Histology / Finding Gross Appearance Key Complication / Clinical Pearl
0 – 4 hours Early coagulative necrosis; wavy fibers (EM); edema begins No gross change — appears normal Troponin starts rising ~3–4h; CK-MB at 4–6h
4 – 24 hours Coagulative necrosis begins; neutrophils start marginating; karyolysis Dark mottling begins V-fib risk (EARLY = ELECTRICAL)
Most common early cause of death
1 – 3 days NEUTROPHILS dominate; loss of nuclei and striations Yellow-tan center forming; hyperemic border begins Fibrinous pericarditis possible (transmural infarct); early rupture risk starts
3 – 7 days ★ MACROPHAGES dominate; phagocytosis of necrotic debris; MMPs digest dead tissue Soft yellow-tan center + bright red hyperemic border MAX RUPTURE RISK — wall weakest before scar forms
Free wall rupture / Papillary rupture / Septal rupture
7 – 14 days Granulation tissue; fibroblasts + neovascularization; inflammation subsiding Red-gray depressed infarct borders Healing phase; Dressler syndrome possible
2+ weeks → months Dense collagenous scar replaces necrotic tissue Gray-white firm scar (depressed) Ventricular aneurysm risk; persistent ST elevation; mural thrombus → embolic stroke
02
Post-MI Complications
Source: Path-3R, Path-5R · All three structural ruptures occur at the same 3–7 day window
Complication Timing Key Clue / Presentation Mechanism
Arrhythmia (V-fib) < 24 hours Sudden cardiac arrest; most common early cause of death Ischemia → electrical instability
Fibrinous pericarditis 1 – 3 days Pleuritic chest pain + friction rub; transmural infarct only Necrosis → pericardial inflammation
LV Free wall rupture 3 – 7 days ★ Sudden collapse; Beck's triad (JVD + hypotension + muffled sounds); cardiac tamponade → death Macrophages digest wall before scar
Papillary muscle rupture 3 – 7 days ★ Flash pulmonary edema; new holosystolic murmur at APEX → radiating to AXILLA Acute MR from macrophage degradation
Septal rupture (VSD) 3 – 7 days ★ New harsh holosystolic murmur at LLSB; acute HF; L→R shunt IVS necrosis → rupture → shunt
Mural thrombus Days – weeks Stasis over akinetic wall → embolic stroke Virchow's triad (stasis + injury)
Dressler syndrome 2 – 10 weeks Fever + pleuritic chest pain + friction rub; negative troponin Anti-heart antibody formation (autoimmune)
Ventricular aneurysm Weeks – months Persistent ST elevation (not reinfarction); dyskinetic wall bulge; mural thrombus risk Fibrotic scar → non-contractile, compliant wall
TRAP: Papillary rupture (apex → axilla) vs Septal rupture (LLSB) — both are holosystolic post-MI. Location is the key differentiator. New troponin elevation = reinfarction, not aneurysm.

Lesion Timing Best Location Radiation LV Response Key Feature
Aortic Stenosis Systolic crescendo-decresc. RUSB (R 2nd ICS) Carotids ★ Concentric LVH (pressure overload) SAD triad; pulsus parvus et tardus; calcific fibrosa
Aortic Regurgitation Early diastolic decresc. LSB (lean forward) None Eccentric LVH (volume overload) Wide pulse pressure; water-hammer pulse; de Musset sign

04
Dynamic Maneuvers
Source: Path-2R, Path-4R · HCM and MVP are the key exceptions
Maneuver Effect on Preload HCM (LVOTO) MVP Click All Other Murmurs Mechanism
Valsalva (strain) / Standing ↓ Decreases preload LOUDER ★ EARLIER Softer ↓ LV volume → ↑ LVOTO in HCM; leaflets prolapse sooner in MVP
Squatting / Handgrip ↑ Increases preload + afterload Softer (less obstruction) LATER Louder ↑ LV volume → less LVOTO; fuller LV → MVP prolapse delayed
Inspiration ↑ Right heart return only No change No change Right-sided murmurs ↑ (TR, PS) ↑ venous return to RV → right-sided valves fill more
Amyl Nitrite (vasodilator) ↓ Afterload + preload Louder Earlier AR/MR softer; AS louder ↓ afterload → ↓ regurgitant gradient; ↑ forward flow stenotic lesions
Step 1 Rule: HCM is THE ONLY common murmur that INCREASES with Valsalva/standing. All others get softer. This is a classic distinguisher. Handgrip (↑ afterload) makes regurgitant murmurs louder, obstructive murmurs softer.

Cardiomyopathy Comparison
Source: Path-4R, Path-5R · EF and mechanism are most tested
Feature Dilated (DCM) Hypertrophic (HCM) Restrictive (RCM)
EF LOW (≤40%) Normal / HIGH Normal (≥50%)
Main problem Systolic dysfunction (contractility) Dynamic LVOTO + diastolic dysfunction Impaired filling (compliance failure)
Gross finding 4-chamber dilation; "flabby heart" >500g Asymmetric septal hypertrophy; "banana" LV cavity Normal wall thickness; biatrial dilation
Classic genetic cause TTN (titin) mutation β-myosin heavy chain (MYH7 / MYBPC3) TTR (transthyretin) amyloid
Classic acquired cause Alcohol / Coxsackie B / doxorubicin / peripartum Autosomal dominant sarcomere mutation Amyloid / sarcoid / hemochromatosis
Histology clue "Ninja star" nuclei (TTN) Myofiber disarray + interstitial fibrosis Congo red → apple-green birefringence (amyloid)
Valsalva on murmur Softer (less flow into dilated LV) LOUDER (↓ LV volume → ↑ LVOTO) No significant murmur
Gallop S3 (dilated, volume overload) S4 (stiff/hypertrophied ventricle) S3 or S4 (impaired filling)
Classic scenario Alcohol use + dilated heart + low EF Young athlete + SCD + ASH on echo Multiple myeloma + "sparkling" echo + Congo red+

09
Heart Failure
Source: cardio-pathology, Path-1Q, Path-1R · HFpEF vs HFrEF distinction is highly tested
Feature Left-Sided HF Right-Sided HF Congestive HF (Both)
Symptoms Dyspnea, orthopnea, PND, crackles, pink frothy sputum JVD, hepatomegaly, peripheral pitting edema, ascites Both pulmonary and systemic congestion
Mnemonic Left = Lungs Right = Rest of body ↑ BNP (ventricular stretch)
Most common cause of RHF Left-sided HF (back-pressure into pulmonary circulation → RV pressure overload)
Gallop S3 = dilated LV (volume overload / HFrEF) S3 or S4 depending on cause S4 = stiff LV (hypertrophy / HFpEF)
Feature HFrEF (Systolic Failure) HFpEF (Diastolic Failure)
EF ≤ 40% — contractility failure ≥ 50% — compliance failure
Main problem LV can't squeeze → ↓ stroke volume LV too stiff to fill → ↑ filling pressures
Classic cause DCM, post-MI, valvular (MR/AR) Hypertension (concentric LVH), HCM, amyloid
Gallop S3 — blood sloshing into dilated LV S4 — blood hitting stiff LV at atrial kick
Treatment GDMT: Beta-blocker + ARNI/ACEi/ARB + MRA + SGLT2i BP/HR control; diuretics for congestion; treat cause
Survival benefit All 4 GDMT pillars proven Diuretics = symptom relief only — no survival benefit in either type
TRAP: Normal EF does NOT mean the heart is functioning normally. HFpEF = preserved EF + diastolic dysfunction + symptoms of HF. A patient with EF 60% can still be in heart failure. Also: never start beta-blockers in acute decompensated HF — only when euvolemic.

Pericardial Disease
Source: cardio-pathology, Path-1R · Constrictive vs restrictive distinction is a classic trap
Condition Buzzwords / Presentation ECG / Imaging Treatment Key Distinguisher
Acute Pericarditis Sharp chest pain; worse lying flat, better leaning forward; pericardial friction rub; viral (Coxsackie) most common Diffuse ST elevation + NO reciprocal changes + PR depression (pathognomonic) NSAIDs + colchicine PR depression distinguishes from STEMI (which has reciprocal changes)
Cardiac Tamponade Beck's Triad: Hypotension + JVD + Muffled heart sounds; pulsus paradoxus >10 mmHg; electrical alternans Electrical alternans on ECG; "water-bottle" silhouette on CXR; swinging heart on echo Urgent pericardiocentesis JVD + hypotension after MI = tamponade from free wall rupture, not HF
Constrictive Pericarditis Chronic impaired filling; Kussmaul sign (JVP rises with inspiration); hepatomegaly; edema Pericardial thickening/calcification on CT/MRI; "square root sign" on cardiac cath Pericardiectomy (surgically curable) Normal pericardium → think RCM; thickened pericardium → think constrictive
Dressler Syndrome Fever + pleuritic chest pain + friction rub 2–10 weeks post-MI; negative troponin Diffuse ST elevation (pericarditis pattern) NSAIDs Autoimmune — anti-heart antibodies. Distinguished from reinfarction by timing + negative troponin
Feature Constrictive Pericarditis Restrictive Cardiomyopathy
Pericardium Thickened / calcified (seen on CT/MRI) Normal pericardium
Kussmaul sign Present (JVP rises with inspiration) May be present but less reliable
Cath finding "Square root sign" (rapid fill → plateau) Similar "dip and plateau" — hard to distinguish
Treatment Surgically curable (pericardiectomy) Treat underlying cause; not surgically curable

Aortic Aneurysms & Dissection
Source: Path-1R (vascular tag) · Type A vs B management is the #1 tested distinction
Condition Classic Scenario Pathology Key Associations Management
Aortic Dissection — Type A Sudden tearing/ripping chest pain radiating to back; older male with chronic HTN Intimal tear in ascending aorta; false lumen; cystic medial degeneration HTN (most common); Marfan / EDS (young patient) Surgical emergency — can rupture into pericardium → tamponade
Aortic Dissection — Type B Same pain pattern but distal to left subclavian Intimal tear in descending aorta HTN; atherosclerosis Medical management — BP + HR control (labetalol first-line)
AAA (Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm) Elderly male smoker; pulsatile abdominal mass; often asymptomatic until rupture Atherosclerosis → medial weakness; infrarenal most common location HTN, smoking, male sex, age >65; screen with ultrasound Surgical repair when >5 cm (rupture risk >25%/yr at >6 cm)
Syphilitic Aortitis Tertiary syphilis; "tree-bark" aorta; aortic regurgitation Vasa vasorum endarteritis → medial ischemia → elastic fiber loss → aneurysm Always ascending aorta; may cause AR from root dilation Treat syphilis; surgical repair of aneurysm if large
Berry Aneurysm "Worst headache of life" + meningismus → subarachnoid hemorrhage Congenital defect of tunica media at Circle of Willis bifurcations ADPKD; coarctation of aorta; Marfan; Ehlers-Danlos Surgical clipping or endovascular coiling
Charcot-Bouchard Microaneurysm Chronic HTN → deep brain hemorrhage (basal ganglia, thalamus); cocaine acute trigger Lipohyalinosis of small penetrating arterioles → microaneurysm formation HTN; cocaine use BP control; supportive for hemorrhage
Aneurysm Location Rules: Berry = Circle of Willis (bifurcations) · Charcot-Bouchard = deep brain (basal ganglia, HTN) · Syphilitic = ascending aorta · AAA = infrarenal aorta (atherosclerosis). These are four distinct entities tested separately.

Vasculitis Differentials
Source: Path-1R (vascular tag) · Organized by vessel size — this is the key organizing framework
Framework: Vasculitis is organized by vessel size. Large = Takayasu / Giant Cell. Medium = PAN / Kawasaki / Buerger. Small = ANCA-associated (Wegener/GPA, MPA, EGPA) + immune complex (HSP, cryoglobulinemia).
Vessel Size Disease Classic Scenario Key Lab / Finding Board Pearl
Large vessel Giant Cell (Temporal) Arteritis Elderly woman >50; headache; jaw claudication; temporal artery tenderness; vision loss risk ↑ ESR; temporal artery biopsy (skip lesions) Treat with steroids before biopsy to prevent blindness; associated with polymyalgia rheumatica
Takayasu Arteritis Young Asian woman; pulseless upper extremities; arm claudication; hypertension ↑ ESR; aortography shows narrowing "Pulseless disease"; granulomatous inflammation of aorta and branches
Medium vessel Polyarteritis Nodosa (PAN) Middle-aged man; fever, weight loss, HTN, renal failure, abdominal pain; hepatitis B association Hepatitis B antigen; angiography shows "string of beads"; ANCA negative Spares pulmonary arteries and glomeruli; no ANCA
Kawasaki Disease Child <5; fever >5 days; CRASH mnemonic (Conjunctivitis, Rash, Adenopathy, Strawberry tongue, Hand/foot changes) ↑ ESR/CRP; echo for coronary aneurysms Leading cause of acquired heart disease in children; treat with IVIG + aspirin
Buerger Disease Young male smoker; superficial thrombophlebitis; distal ulcers; Raynaud; segmental limb occlusions Clinical diagnosis; angiography shows segmental occlusions Non-atherosclerotic, non-autoimmune; STOP SMOKING is the only treatment
Small vessel
(ANCA-assoc.)		 GPA (Wegener) Upper + lower respiratory tract + kidneys; saddle-nose deformity; hemoptysis; hematuria c-ANCA (PR3) positive Classic triad: sinusitis + pulmonary nodules + glomerulonephritis
MPA (Microscopic Polyangiitis) Pulmonary-renal syndrome; no upper respiratory involvement; no granulomas p-ANCA (MPO) positive Distinguishable from GPA by lack of upper respiratory / granulomas
EGPA (Churg-Strauss) Asthma + eosinophilia + vasculitis; late-onset asthma in adults p-ANCA (MPO); ↑ eosinophils Asthma + eosinophilia = EGPA until proven otherwise
Small vessel
(immune complex)		 HSP (IgA Vasculitis) Child; palpable purpura on buttocks/legs; arthritis; GI pain; hematuria; follows URI IgA deposits on biopsy; normal platelets Most common vasculitis in children; IgA nephropathy connection
Fibromuscular Dysplasia Young woman; secondary HTN; renal bruit; "string of pearls" on angiography Angiography (string of pearls pattern) Non-inflammatory, non-atherosclerotic; medial hyperplasia → RAAS activation
If You See Think Vasculitis Key Differentiator
Elderly woman + jaw claudication + vision loss Giant Cell Arteritis Treat steroids BEFORE biopsy to preserve vision
Young Asian woman + pulseless arms Takayasu Arteritis "Pulseless disease" — aortic arch branches
Child + fever 5 days + CRASH features Kawasaki Disease Coronary aneurysm risk — IVIG + aspirin
Hep B + HTN + renal failure + NO ANCA + spares lungs Polyarteritis Nodosa Medium vessel; no ANCA; no pulmonary involvement
Upper + lower resp. + kidneys + c-ANCA GPA (Wegener) Saddle-nose; c-ANCA = PR3
Asthma + eosinophilia + p-ANCA EGPA (Churg-Strauss) Asthma is the sentinel feature
Young male smoker + distal ulcers + thrombophlebitis Buerger Disease Stop smoking = only treatment
Child + purpura on buttocks + GI pain + hematuria HSP / IgA Vasculitis IgA deposits; follows URI; most common vasculitis in children
